Language Movement

February 21st is approaching. International Mother Language Day. A day the whole world recognizes, but only we, Bangladeshis, truly understand the weight of.

Most people outside Bangladesh know this day as UNESCO's celebration of linguistic diversity. They might see posts about it, maybe their country does a small event. But they don't know the blood. They don't know the sacrifice. They don't know that this international day exists because of a group of students who were shot dead on the streets of Dhaka for demanding to speak their mother tongue.

This post is for them.

For readers outside Bangladesh: What follows is real history. The dates, the names, the events; all true. February 21 isn't just a "cultural appreciation day" for us. It's Shaheed Dibosh. Martyr's Day.

1952: The Day Everything Changed

To understand February 21, you have to understand 1947.

That's when India was partitioned and Pakistan was created. But Pakistan wasn't one unified land, it was two halves, West Pakistan and East Pakistan, separated by 1,500 kilometers of India. Different languages, different cultures, different everything. The western part spoke Urdu. The eastern part (now Bangladesh) spoke Bengali.

In 1948, Pakistan's leader Muhammad Ali Jinnah came to Dhaka and declared that Urdu would be the only state language of Pakistan. Not Bengali. Just Urdu. He said it was necessary for national unity.

Imagine someone telling you that your mother tongue, the language your grandmother sings lullabies in, the language of your poetry, your jokes, your very identity, is not allowed. That you must abandon it and adopt another.

The students of Dhaka University refused.

For four years, they protested. They organized. They demanded that Bengali be given equal status. The government responded with bans on public gatherings, with arrests, with force.

Then came February 21, 1952.

The Day Itself

The government had imposed Section 144 (a law banning public assemblies.) But the students decided to defy it. At 9 AM, they began gathering at Dhaka University. By afternoon, thousands had assembled.

They marched toward the legislative assembly to present their demands.

The police blocked their path. They warned them to disperse. The students kept moving forward.

Then the shooting started.

Salam was among the first to fall. Then Rafiq. Then Barkat. Then Jabbar. Teenagers and young men, their bodies crumpling on the street, their blood staining the ground. Official records say four died that day. Many believe the number was higher.

"Amar bhaiyer rakte rangano Ekushey February, ami ki bhulite pari?"

(Can I forget the twenty-first of February, stained with my brothers' blood?)

This song plays everywhere in Bangladesh on February 21. On every radio station. In every procession. Every child learns it in school. Every adult can sing it from memory.

The Aftermath

The deaths didn't stop the movement. They intensified it.

In 1954, four years after the massacre, the government finally relented. Bengali was granted official status alongside Urdu. It was a victory, but it came at a terrible cost.

Many historians say the Language Movement was the first step toward Bangladesh's independence. The seed was planted in 1952. It grew through the decades until 1971, when we finally became a free nation.

1948
Jinnah declares in Dhaka: "Urdu, and only Urdu shall be the state language of Pakistan."
1952
February 21: Students protest. Police open fire. Salam, Rafiq, Barkat, Jabbar are killed.
1956
Bengali finally recognized as a state language of Pakistan.
1971
Bangladesh becomes independent. The Language Movement is seen as the beginning.
1999
UNESCO declares February 21 International Mother Language Day — proposed by Bangladesh, recognized by the world.

From Dhaka to the World

Here's what many don't know: International Mother Language Day exists because Bangladesh proposed it to UNESCO. Rafiqul Islam, a Bengali living in Canada, wrote a letter to Kofi Annan asking for a day to honor the 1952 martyrs. Bangladesh formally submitted the proposal. In 1999, UNESCO declared February 21 as International Mother Language Day.

So when you see posts about linguistic diversity on February 21, remember: that's because of us. Because of Salam, Rafiq, Barkat, Jabbar. Because students died in Dhaka.

The world calls it International Mother Language Day. We call it Ekushey. Now you know why.

The Shaheed Minar

After the killings, students built a temporary monument at the spot where the martyrs fell. The police demolished it almost immediately. But people kept building it back. Finally, a permanent Shaheed Minar (Martyr's Monument) was constructed.

Its design is intentional. The tall structure represents a mother standing with her fallen sons. The small railings symbolize the barriers the martyrs broke through. The red sun behind it represents the blood and the new dawn.

Every year on February 21, people walk barefoot to the Shaheed Minar. They carry flowers. They sing songs. They stand in silence. It happens at the main monument in Dhaka and at every Shaheed Minar across the country.

My Personal Plan

Here's my situation this year.

I'm not in university yet. Still trying to get in. Still preparing, hoping, applying. I graduated from college, so I can't join the college rallies either. I'm in that weird in-between space where I don't belong to any institutional group.

At first I was frustrated. Felt left out. Watched friends make plans while I wondered where I fit.

Then I realized something important: you don't need an institution to pay respects. There are always individual rallies. People gather at different Shaheed Minars across the city. Small groups, families, old people who've been doing this for decades. Anyone can join. Everyone is welcome.

So that's my plan. Find a local Shaheed Minar. Walk barefoot with whoever's there. Place flowers. Stand in silence. Remember.

Because honestly? It's a powerful experience. There's something about walking barefoot on cold pavement, surrounded by strangers who share your respect, singing songs you've known since childhood. It connects you to something bigger.

What Outsiders Don't Know

I want to be clear about something.

In Bangladesh, everyone knows this history. Every child learns it in school. Every adult can name the martyrs. We grow up with "Amar Bhaiyer Rakte Rangano" playing every February. We know why we walk barefoot. We know why we carry flowers.

But outside Bangladesh? It's different.

I've talked to people from other countries who celebrate International Mother Language Day without knowing why it's February 21. They think UNESCO just picked a random date. They don't know about Salam and Rafiq. They don't know about the bullets and the blood.

That's not their fault. How would they know? It's our history, our story to tell.

So this post is for them. For anyone reading from outside Bangladesh who wants to understand what this day really means.

"People won't know the value of what they have until it's being taken away from them."

That's the core of it, isn't it? Most of the world takes their mother language for granted because no one ever tried to take it. We don't have that luxury. We remember what it cost to keep ours.

What I'm Doing This Year

February 21, 2026. Here's the plan:

  • Wake up early. Before sunrise.
  • Find a local Shaheed Minar. There's one not too far from my neighborhood.
  • Join whatever group gathers there. Doesn't matter if I know anyone.
  • Walk barefoot. Feel the ground. Remember who walked here before.
  • Place flowers. Say a prayer. Not religious, just respectful.
  • Listen to the songs. Sing along, even if my voice cracks.

And after that? Share this post. Tell more people. Make sure the story reaches beyond our borders.

Quick Facts for Non-Bangladeshis

  • What is it? International Mother Language Day / Shaheed Day (Martyr's Day)
  • When? February 21, every year
  • Why 1952? Students in Dhaka were killed protesting for Bengali language rights
  • Who died? Salam, Barkat, Rafiq, Jabbar, and others
  • Why global? UNESCO declared it International Mother Language Day in 1999 — proposed by Bangladesh
  • What do people do? Walk barefoot to Shaheed Minar, place flowers, sing songs, stand in silence
  • What's a Shaheed Minar? Martyr's Monument, built to honor those who died
